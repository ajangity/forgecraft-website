Background

From Robotics Labs to Building the Future of Products

I'm studying robotics engineering at UC Santa Cruz — a program that puts you at the intersection of hardware and software, building systems that have to work in the physical world where "almost correct" isn't good enough.

That mindset shapes everything I build. I care about systems that are verifiably correct, not just plausible. I care about the full pipeline — from problem statement to delivered product. And I care about building things that actually help people, not just impressive demos.

Robotics taught me that the hardest problems aren't in any single component. They're in the integration — making all the parts work together reliably, under conditions you didn't fully anticipate. That's exactly the problem ForgeCraft solves.

BananaBots

Shipping Real Products for Real Users

BananaBots is the engineering organization I co-founded and co-lead at UC Santa Cruz. Our mission is to build assistive technology — real products that improve quality of life for people with disabilities, not research prototypes that sit in a drawer.

Running BananaBots taught me more about building products under constraints than any class could. Limited budget, limited time, diverse team, real users with real needs — that's the environment where I learned to ship.

It also taught me how frustrating it is when the tools don't fit the problem. We constantly needed custom software for specific BananaBots workflows — data dashboards, tracking systems, onboarding tools — and building each one from scratch consumed time we needed for the actual product work. That frustration is what ForgeCraft is designed to eliminate.

Why ForgeCraft

The Problem I Keep Running Into

Every team I've worked with — BananaBots, freelance projects, personal ideas — has faced the same wall: you need a tool, the tools that exist don't quite fit, and building a custom one costs too much or takes too long.

For most people, that's where the idea dies. Not because it wasn't good. Not because there wasn't demand. But because the cost and complexity of turning an idea into software is too high to justify.

I think that's a solvable problem. The AI models that exist today can reason about software architecture, write code, and verify outputs. The infrastructure to run it exists. What's missing is a pipeline that wraps all of that into an experience anyone can use — regardless of technical background.

That's what ForgeCraft is. Not a chatbot that writes code you then have to debug. A full pipeline that takes your idea and delivers a working product.
